User Flow Document

GachiWork — A Peer Network for E-9 Workers

An industry-focused peer network connecting E-9 foreign workers in Korea. AI removes language gaps and carries accumulated colleague experiences across the network, becoming more intelligent over time. This document maps the 4-phase user journey from first contact to active participation.

Before — AI focused "A community that uses AI for translation and RAG for answering."
After — Network focused "An industry-focused peer network. AI removes language gaps and transfers accumulated experiences to colleagues, becoming more intelligent over time."
Flow 01

Onboarding — From install to joining the network

Onboarding routes users into their industry peer network in the fewest possible steps, built around the language environment of foreign workers.

The gap E-9 workers are confined to assigned workplaces, and same-industry peers are scattered across the country. With workers from 17 countries, language further blocks access to informal information. Placing native-language setup and industry selection at the very start turns the first screen into an exit from isolation.

App install

Through peer recommendation, QR posters at worksites, or guidance during mandatory employment training.

Native-language selection

All UI, notifications, and AI responses are delivered in the chosen language from this point forward.

Quick signup — phone verification

Phone verification only. No email, no extensive personal information.

Industry selection

Manufacturing, agriculture & livestock, fisheries, or services — the choice becomes the key for peer network matching.

Entering the industry network

The feed for the chosen industry appears. Peer questions and answers, along with AI responses, are all shown in the user's native language.

Flow 02

Core usage — Peer exchange, enabled by AI

Workers post in their native language. AI auto-translates to every industry peer; when the post is detected as a question, AI posts an auto-reply in the thread. Peers add their own replies over time — all back-translated into the questioner's language.

The gap Frontline questions — whether dormitory deductions are reasonable, whether overtime can be refused — are informal by nature and have no official answer channel. Only same-industry peers can share that experiential knowledge, yet the 17-country language gap made reaching those peers impossible.

Question posted in native language

Example: 🇲🇲 "Is a 300,000 KRW dormitory deduction normal?"

AI auto-translation — across the 17 main languages

The post is translated in real time into the primary languages of 17 origin countries — Vietnamese, Nepali, Indonesian, and others — so every peer in the same industry sees it in their own language.

Implementation — Multilingual real-time translation An LLM-based translation API starts from a general-purpose baseline, then gradually adapts to industry-specific vocabulary (injection molding, press, dormitory deductions) and frontline expressions accumulated in the community — pushing contextual accuracy beyond what a generic translator offers.
Structural fix for the 17-language gap

Broadcast across the industry network

Because the network is organized by industry rather than by language, the question reaches every peer in the same sector regardless of nationality.

🤖 AI auto-reply — when the post is a question

An AI agent classifies each post. If it is detected as a question, AI posts an auto-reply in the same thread, just like any other comment. The reply draws from two sources depending on the question type.

Daily-life questions

A general LLM summarizes accumulated peer experiences via RAG. The answer grows more accurate as more community experiences accumulate.

Legal / sensitive questions

Answers are grounded in official documents (labor law, SOPs) for accuracy, with a link to the official source.

Implementation — Question detection + RAG A lightweight classifier tags each new post as question / non-question. For questions, a RAG pipeline retrieves the most relevant peer experiences and official documents from a vector database, then generates a reply in the questioner's native language. Peer reactions to the AI reply feed back into the store — the network becomes more intelligent as it grows.

👥 Peer replies accumulate in the thread

Peers from across the 17 countries add their own experiences as separate replies, each written in their own language — posted alongside the AI reply in the same thread.
Example: 🇻🇳 "Ours is 200,000"

All answers back-translated to the questioner's language

Answers written across many native languages are automatically converted into the questioner's language, making the exchange feel like a conversation between speakers of a single language.

Worker reaches a judgment

With peer experience (a real-world benchmark) and legal grounds (the official standard) side by side, the worker can judge whether their situation is within normal range.

Flow 03

Rights protection — From signal detection to expert connection

Risk signals surface during ordinary community activity. Without having to find a separate reporting channel, the path to resolution stays continuous within the platform.

The gap Workers facing wage theft or mistreatment often have no native-language guidance for filing a report, and fear of employer retaliation leads many to abandon the official channels. A peer's lived experience is a far more actionable basis for action than a translated manual, but isolation blocks that path.

Ordinary community post

A worker shares a question or experience. Anonymous posting is supported given the risk of employer retaliation.
Example: "My salary hasn't come in for 3 months — is this normal?"

AI risk-signal detection

An AI agent analyzes post text, recognizing keywords and contextual patterns tied to wage theft, safety incidents, or mistreatment.

Implementation — Risk-detection agent A dedicated detection agent runs independently of translation and search. Beyond keyword matching, it reads contextual patterns (e.g., duration + non-payment) and continuously improves its precision by referencing the accumulated case history.

Native-language rights guidance

The worker receives — as a private notification, not a public post — guidance in their native language on remediation procedures and relevant agencies (Ministry of Employment and Labor, Foreign Workforce Support Centers, National Human Rights Commission).

In-platform guidance, no separate channel needed

AI first-line consultation

The AI summarizes the situation and explains applicable statutes in the worker's native language, grounded in official legal sources.
Example: "Under Article 36 of the Labor Standards Act, you can claim unpaid wages within 14 days of separation."

Escalation when needed

When AI consultation alone is not enough, two paths open to the worker.

Expert connection

A direct in-platform connection to labor attorneys and other professionals.

Peer experience

The AI surfaces community posts from peers who resolved similar situations.

Flow 04

Retention — From member to active contributor

Onboarding brings a worker into the network, but what turns a passive viewer into an active contributor is the first post and the first answer received. This flow traces that transition and the self-reinforcing loop it creates.

The gap A network is only valuable with active members, not just visitors. Most workers who onboard will browse peer content quietly; what pushes someone to post, answer, and return is the realization that "someone in my situation is here" — followed by the value of the first exchange they receive.

Passive browsing in the network

After onboarding, the worker browses peer posts in their industry. The realization forms: "Someone in my situation is here."

First question posted

The worker writes their own question in native language. This moment marks the transition from information consumer to network participant.

Peer + AI answers received

Peer answers arrive alongside AI-generated summaries. The worker experiences the network's value firsthand.

Active network member

New questions and answering others' questions follow. The worker settles in as an active member.

Overview

Full user journey — how the 4 flows connect

The four flows are one continuous journey. A worker onboards (Flow 01), engages in core exchange (Flow 02), branches to rights protection if needed (Flow 03), and settles into active network membership (Flow 04) — feeding the self-reinforcing loop that makes the network more intelligent over time.
